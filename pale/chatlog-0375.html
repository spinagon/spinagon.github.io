Specifically, we were reading court cases and IRS documents surrounding what the U.S. government defines as 'religion'. This is for my class on Religion in its Global Context. A lot of what we're doing in both of my religion classes so far has been comparing the construction of 'religion' to institutions we don't call religions and determining the nature of their relationships- so for example 'what does the court system define as a religion'. To which the answer is (and typically has been) 'things which resemble Christianity enough'. It was the case for Buddhism in the early 19th century, and it's the case for organizations today- like how Scientology uses a cross as an aesthetic signifier, possibly with the motivation of being legitimized by the government. We also talk about religion as an 'alternative to the state'- a paradoxical idea in some sense, since the state is ever-present -and how the institution of 'religious freedom' in many ways undercuts practice as a part of religion in favor of the very protestant ideals of belief as a keystone of religion. Another thing we're exploring is how the government has pushed back on movements that it's deemed too dangerous, such as when the city of Philadelphia dropped an experimental bomb on the home of the MOVE group (killing 11 people) and later refused its survivors religious recognition in prison.

JayManiac 1/24/2022 6:15 PM
Capitalism as Religion is actually something that Marx and Weber (and a lot of Marxists and uh...Weber-ists?) spend a lot of time discussing. Weber attributes lots of horrible capitalist practices to the Protestant Work Ethic- a view which I largely agree with -and then Marx coined the term 'commodity fetishism' to refer to the way that brands will artificially inflate their perceived value beyond the factors by which he believed commodity should be valued (that is 'how useful is it to people' and 'how much labor was required to produce it').
6:17 PM
His game plan was to apply the anthropological term of a 'fetish' (similar to an idol) to capitalist systems, because that term was being used to discuss 'backwards' religions in africa at the time, and he wanted to point out that capitalist systems were no different, using the mechanisms of secularization and eurocentric elitism to cultivate a disgust for capitalism.
6:20 PM
I have a lot of complicated feelings about that. Because I wholeheartedly believe that capitalism is functioning on many of the same axes as 'religions' or 'superstitions' that Marx compared it to. But also the categories of religion and superstition were created during colonialism to justify a lot of hatred and undermine a lot of indigenous systems and worldviews, and secularization in many ways is the next step in that process.
6:23 PM
Plenty of modern 'atheists' are still very much culturally christian in how they behave and the values that they uphold- but the protestant centralization of 'belief' has created this illusion that by doing away with your literal belief in the Christian God you can somehow be free of that 'religion'.
6:23 PM
It's a very complicated topic, and also one of my favorite topics (if you couldn't tell).

Sengachi | Mess Of Math & Dreams 1/24/2022 8:14 PM
There's also the more complicated element of morality and the trolly problem, where Lucy's decision is part of a heuristic that has to be considered in the broader context of her life. Because human interpersonal interactions and moral decision making is just way too complicated (and often time crunched) for us to cleanly evaluate every component of a situation and make an optimal decision. So we use heuristics that are a decent enough choice for the context of our lives, most of the time. (Heck this is true for how human sapience/intelligence works in general). So it's impossible to evaluate Lucy's decision not to kill Charles in a way that's disconnected from the rest of her life, where she doesn't have to make decisions like this on life and death, and where such serious irreversible decisions instead of seeking third options would be wrong more often than not. But of course that's, ironically, not how humans evaluate our own moral decision making. We do self-evaluate and judge based on the outcomes of our decisions in specific circumstances, because the full context of our moral reasoning over the course of our lives is too complicated to cleanly evaluate. So Lucy's stuck berating herself for this decision in its isolated context, when she made the decision with a heuristic that's actually quite good for the rest of her life.
