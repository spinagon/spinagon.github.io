That's a really interesting framing of it, and the Taylor situation is a really great example. It's interesting to think of the implicit contract as an illusion, because on one hand it is an illusion, it's the great illusion of adulthood: that once you become an adult or parent you have your shit together, and if that illusion doesn't shatter at some point throughout childhood then it's a bit of a rude awakening in adulthood when that miracle transformation into someone who knows what to do just...doesn't happen. But also: one thing my dad drilled into me when I was starting to wander outside those bounds was that I was pretty bad at risk assessment.
8:47 PM
And in that sense the 'got it all together' illusion is an illusion like this story is an illusion: it's not true that parents can always handle problems better than kids, or that kids can't be reasonably self-sufficient, but that story stands in for truths that people (kids and adults alike) aren't super capable of wrapping their heads around: our environment and its visible attributes do not correlate to the risk that one is undergoing. You have never been in a car crash, nobody you know has, even though you consistently drive 20 over the limit, driving with your knees to impress your friends. This does not in fact mean that you are at low risk for a car crash.
