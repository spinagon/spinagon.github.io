Back as promised, and it's storytime! Specifically story-within-a-story time, which is one of my favorite kinds of storytime. Especially when it comes to the Otherverse. “I can recall a twice-knotted temple on a wintery peak, accessible only through passage between two mountains, a day’s journey, and passage through a cave. Travel that far, the temperature dipping with every minute spent out in the snowstorms, and you can find a mountain surrounded by a crown of lesser mountains, with a temple built atop it. The temple was built by spirits at the behest of an ogre magi. Do you know what an ogre magi is?”I do! Vaguely anyhow; it'll be real nice to have some context. The reason I said this reminded me of my homework for my 'Spirits Ghosts and Monsters' class is that a whole lot of the stories start out like this, specifically in the mountains, in the 'unknown' as it were, where oni and dragon and yokai realms abound and the only familiar things are often the temples, shrines, the odd parallels to human culture. This very much fits the vibe of 'natural area unconquered by humanity' and I'm also thinking this is somewhere in siberia, probably because of the Soviet Union parallels that I just mentioned. It's also going to be really interesting to see how exactly Rook kept fighting the Oni wars in Europe, or even how Oni behave over there. There's also, of course, the idea of a twice-knotted place, which would mean that there's an Other realm which has enough of its own culture and pull that it can hold its shape even when another part of it starts to knot into something else, which is super duper cool. I wonder how often these twice-knotted places come into being? By its nature, not something you can really study, but it's cool in any case, especially since we already see how differently things work here, with spirits themselves having the influence and materiality to build temples, which wouldn't be the case in modern-unknotted times.

“Ogres are much like men, but more brutish in some ways, less brutish in others. The word ogre derives from Orcus, an Etruscan deity of death, but their origins are wider. They are massive Others resembling men, content to dine on humans, but Death, War, Nature, Fate, or other forces have swelled in them and made them great in stature. They may be bleak, violent, bestial, or important, respectively. They are often simple, prone to rhythms and patterns in keeping with their nature. These rhythms and patterns can be exploited.” “That’s unfortunate.”Yeah, the etymology of ogres and what has been considered an ogre...well, it's almost as wide as what's considered a goblin. Examples include Grendel from Beowulf, but also more specific ones like the cyclopes of greek mythology or even the jotunn (which I still maintain would be more their own thing in the Otherverse, related to the fae). The fun thing about the Otherverse is how it integrates all these things from a multitude of cultures into a unified system which works on itself. For example here, the ogre are defined by various forces, accounting for their varied nature. Wildbow's pretty great at making a format for a kind of creature and then riffing on it and making dozens of variations.
