Less so is the context of this opening up, which is dealing with this immense, heavy political conflict in a very mundane way, showing how these potential microaggressions affect someone, where the cycle is pushed. Because at some point, Lucy responds to this subliminal bias by being rude, refusing to listen, and defending herself and her lifestyle in that aggressive way, and- while she's not unjustified there -it only exacerbates the issue, since it gives him more reasons to go after her. I think this is the best way to tackle this kind of thing in media: -aim for empathy and understanding of how the situation affects people. We understand here how one person is subconsciously biased, and how Lucy notices and is hurt by that. -don't make it the whole character struggle, but don't leave it out entirely. Society's issues become personal issues, but in a way that makes sense and is personalized to the character, playing on their worldview and development.

This also demonstrates very clearly that good representation that gets people to think about these issues and makes a difference in their thought process doesn't have to be...huge, or shocking. You don't have to showcase extreme racial violence, or have the n word 110 times in your movie to make it 'real'. Those sides of things are important, but they're not the only ways- or even effective ways, for some audiences -to change hearts. It can be as simple as making us care about a character for a huge multitude of reasons, and then showing us how they're affected in even the smallest ways. Not that that's...necessarily simple- in fact it's much easier to write people shouting slurs at each other, a lot of the time -but nonetheless, I find it much more effective.
