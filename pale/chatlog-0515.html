“We’re animals,” Lucy said. “We’re… animals with a few billion years of evolution and thousands of years of trying to form society, sometimes taking us down the wrong paths, or giving us stuff that- maybe it helped in the moment, but it fucks us up today. Appeals to evolution are something I take with a good amount of scrutiny anyways because of how misused they are in discourse in general and Lucy's take on it isn't that much better. The tendency of contemporary society to be patriarchal for example, is not an evolutionary inevitability...it's just culture, it's just how things are right now, we're taught those norms, they don't just exist in our dna. Lactose intolerance is evolutionary, but it's got a traceable history too...and even framing it as a disorder is odd when in some sense it's the default- Lucy starts the paragraph with 'we're animals' few other mammals tolerate milk as long as some humans do, you could almost reframe it as 'those populations, due to their cultural practice, developed a milk tolerance'. It might seem like I'm nitpicking here- at the end of the day Lucy and Verona are both saying that our past has shaped a world that isn't working -but I really think there's a substantial difference here even beyond the fact that Lucy's appeal to nature strikes at a particular pet peeve of mine. Verona identifies a driving philosophy of society, specific problem, and specific victims...Lucy's elaboration identifies vague problems as these sorts of human inevitabilities and doesn't adequately connect them to the social issues she's talking about, which kind of signals to me that she hasn't thought this through.
