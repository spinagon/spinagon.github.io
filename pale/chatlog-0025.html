I agree that it's kinda brainwashy to make a kid swear to inherit a demesne, but mostly in the "you're ten, maybe you shouldn't be agreeing to any lifelong commitments because your family wants you to" way. Is it really worse than swearing a kid to a familiar bond? Sure, there are possibilities for a toxic arranged familiar bond to turn out okay, but there are options to do that with a demesne as well - it just becomes a question of learning to work with your family's legacy and wishes instead of the wishes of whatever entity they bonded you off to. Forcing a child to follow in their family's footsteps is bad, but that's mostly because of the "forcing" part. On the other hand, if we get to the point where the practitioner heir is capable of making informed lifelong commitments and they have the option to claim an inherited demesne, that can absolutely be a reasonable choice. It would mean limiting yourself to mostly stay within the boundaries of your family's legacy, but I'd see it as akin to taking a position in the family business - if you hate the job, then don't do it, but if it mostly lines up with what you want to be doing then it might be worth accepting certain restrictions in exchange for a huge leg up.
4:26 AM
re: inheritance - it does become less personal, but that doesn't necessarily mean not personal, sort of like inheriting a non-practice place. I chose Diana for this example specifically because even leaving aside power considerations, I think she might genuinely prefer a place of power that is literally (in the way that the Practice makes metaphors literal) partially a remnant of Doug over a place that was entirely of Diana.
