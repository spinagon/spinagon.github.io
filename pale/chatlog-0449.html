Sengachi | Mess Of Math & Dreams 6/11/2022 2:26 AM
I feel like most urban fantasy only lets the parents in reluctantly though, with the exception of cases where it's important for the plot that they always knew. It's a reality the story is kind of forced to engage with, but one that runs contrary to its fundamental concept. Some stories handle that dissonance well, but it's still often dissonance. But the key difference between most of these stories and Pale, is that while in Pale obviously the girls are reluctant to tell their parents, the narrative is not. By the time this happens in Pale, the reader has spent many arcs wishing the kids could lean on their parents about this. The story is very abundantly clear that their parents were really should know about this and that it would be for the best if they did. Them learning about it is a problem to navigate, but not a problem in of itself. (edited)
Sengachi | Mess Of Math & Dreams 6/11/2022 2:42 AM
. When the trio have been interacting with their parents (Verona with Sylvia specifically, but that's a given) and unable to talk to them about the hidden magical world, it's not meant to ramp up the tension because "ohhhh, the parents can't know". It ramps up the tension because goddamnit they should know. This artificial line of Innocence is causing damage in this relationship, depriving these kids of their parents' support, creating a living nightmare scenario for the parents, and we want them to know. It's not about "what if the parents knew?" spoiling the fun, it's about "the parents don't know" spoiling the relationship. That's the difference which makes Pale so different in this regard.
