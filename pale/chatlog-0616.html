Yeah like ... let's use a wildly less serious example. Let's say you learned your kid had been, without your knowledge, getting radicalized by the alt-right online. Or that they've become a bully and they're tormenting the kids on their swim team. Obviously you would want to open that conversation with discussion and attempts at understanding. But these are matters which deal with real and present harms. Children around your kid are going to get hurt if nothing is done. At some point, unilaterally banning or restricting internet access, or taking them off the swim team and limiting their group activities to those supervised by someone you trust are necessary. Because if you don't do that, you are responsible for the harms your child is going to do to others. Shouldn't the same apply when the child your kid might harm is themself? No matter how well intentioned the cause for which it would happen? Parents have a duty to prevent harm when it comes to children. It so happens that treating teenagers like people who can listen and understand and internalize what you say, and have a meaningful input about their own lives, is happily the best way to do that most of the time. And most of the time people who disagree with that are simply refusing to incorporate self-criticism or trust into their parenting worldview. But. That's kind of like saying the same is true of adults. It is. Most of the time. And yet we recognize that not all situations with adults can be resolved entirely by words. (See: Charles) So there are definitely scenarios where, after discussion has failed, unilateral exertion of what authority a parent has is definitely the right decision. The question then is, if your child being shot doesn't warrant that, what does? (edited)
