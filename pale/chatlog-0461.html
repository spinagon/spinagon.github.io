The general idea about Undertale, from what I remember, is that in-world it socially treats the standard RPG violence as murder, if you choose to engage in it. If a creature-person accosts you in a tunnel and says "you shall not pass" and then you kill them over it, the other denizens of the world treat you as if you just showed up in their community and murdered someone. A key part of the gameplay is that some characters cause you problems and hassle you and obstruct your progress, and regardless, killing them is seen as murder. The game did a fairly good job using that to deconstruct the typical RPG framework, where the solution to that would be murder. Especially how it primes you to default to murder and then experience the consequences for that simply by looking like a typical RPG. Though while I think the game holds up as a critique of RPG default assumptions around violence, I think the premise holds up a little less well strictly on its own merit. There are minor filler enemies who randomly come to and attack you with lethal violence (seriously, lava blasts are one example) and using violence to defend yourself against that, including getting an unlucky crit which kills them on accident, is treated the same as if you walk up to someone in the town square and execute them. So while the game is good at making you reflect on how customary and automatic violence is as a solution in RPGs, it doesn't actually do a good job of presenting you with a world where violence is genuinely an inappropriate response.
