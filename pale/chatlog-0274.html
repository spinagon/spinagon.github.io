I was trying to summarize a psychological experiment that demonstrated that people are much more conservative in preserving what they already have more willing to take chances to get something when they don't have anything yet. But I can't remember the details of how they rigged it. It involved telling each half of the experiment different info about the start conditions, but each group got a choice that amounted to the same thing: Either take a certain chance to get $1000 or a 50/50 chance of getting $2000 or nothing. The trick was that one group was told that their start condition was that they had nothing and they could take the 1K or gamble on getting nothing or the 2K The second group was told they already had the 1K and they could gamble the 1K with a 50/50 chance of getting another 1K but they'd lose the initial 1K if it went against them. It was based in some story about convoluted inheritance and legal maneuvers. In each presentation, the choices have identical consequences. A sure 1000 or an even chance of 0 or 2000. But the ones that were told that they already had 1000 in the inheritance and could gamble it versus anther 1000 were more likely to take the sure 1000. The ones that were told that they hadn't gotten any inheritance and they could either get a sure 1000 or gamble on 2000 were more likely to go for the two. This is because the ones that had been told they already had the 1000 were trying to conserve what they "already had."
