Rambling thoughts about old stuff that's probably been discussed at the time but I can't remember the discussion, so I might be rethreading stuff that's already been said: After reading this chapter I was reminded of toward the start of the story, the Awakening ritual, and how you were like "dammit they were so close" when they went with the "traditional" wood instead of the "modern" metal, because you wanted them to detach themselves from the toxic mindset of traditional Practitioners. (At least I think that was you?). And I think it's coming back to me after these few chapters, because I can finally articulate why I instinctively disagreed, why wood felt right to me. We've been told that wood is a dying elements being supplanted by metal from textbooks. From Practitioners commenting on it. Modern Practitioner society, including the modern generations of traditional families, is the one saying that wood is a dying element and that now metal is in. The Thorburn's awakening ritual used metal. Traditional Practitioner families have probably been awakening using metal for a few generations already. Fell said wood is never used for awakening, because metal supplanted it post-industrialization.
7:56 PM
Musser represent everything wrong with modern Practice, and it might be tempting to point at the fact that he's the magical equivalent of old money (as well as literally being old money) as the (wooden) root of the problem. But I think that as "traditional" as the family is, it... calcified over time, changing with the times while staying set in its ways. Because the Musser's family's traditional allegiance isn't to tradition. It's to power. And so I think it makes a lot of sense for them to discard the "dying" wood and adopt metal in their awakening. Metal represents things like industry, but it's static in a way. Once you've built something out of metal, you just keep maintaining it until you can build the more efficient (cheaper, more productive) version, and then the previous version is at best recycled, at worse left to rot. Wood is alive, wood grows, trees take what they need from the ground around them, but they also give back. Wood represents nature. I'm reminded of a quote from Untouchables: "if you're worried about grabbing a rotten apple, don't take from the basket, pick one from the tree" (or something in that vein). The Kenneteers selecting wood is "traditional" but they're going back to the fundamental origin of the Practice: learning and deriving power directly from the Others. It's so basic, I wouldn't even call it a tradition. It's a seed. And from that seed they have the freedom to grow into something new instead of following the cold metallic mentality of modern Practice.
